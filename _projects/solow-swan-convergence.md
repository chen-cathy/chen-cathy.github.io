##  Research Overview

This study applies the **Solow-Swan capital accumulation model** to analyze the development trajectories of four economies from 2005 to 2024 and tests the economic convergence hypothesis.

### Research Subjects
- Czech Republic
- Taiwan
- Singapore
- South Korea

### Core Research Questions
1. How does capital accumulation drive long-term economic growth?
2. Do poorer economies grow faster than richer ones? (β-convergence)
3. Are economic gaps between countries narrowing? (σ-convergence)

---

## 🔬 Theoretical Framework

### The Solow-Swan Model

Long-term economic growth stems from three main sources:
1. **Capital Accumulation** (more machinery and infrastructure)
2. **Labor Force Growth** (more workers)
3. **Technological Progress** (more efficient production methods)

**Key Concepts:**
- Diminishing returns to capital accumulation
- Steady state equilibrium
- Only technological progress (g) provides sustained long-term growth

**Basic Equation:** y(t) = y(0) × e^(gt)

---

## 📈 Main Findings

### 1. β-Convergence Test

**Hypothesis:** Poorer economies should grow faster and "catch up" to richer ones.

**Result:**  **No convergence found**

- No evidence of an automatic "catch-up" mechanism
- Each economy follows its own unique development path
- Initial conditions do not determine growth rates

### 2. σ-Convergence Test

**Hypothesis:** The gap between rich and poor economies should narrow over time.

**Coefficient of Variation Analysis:**
- Linear trend: y = 0.0017x + 0.5111, R² = 0.27
- Polynomial trend: R² = 0.33
- Low R² indicates high volatility with no clear trend

**Standard Deviation Analysis (ln GDP per capita):**
- Linear trend: y = 0.0027x + 0.4495, **R² = 0.57**
- Standard deviation increased from 0.4587 to 0.4960
- **Rising trend is significant**

**Result:**  **No convergence; slight divergence observed**

##  Case Study: Taiwan (1971-2024)

### Model Comparison

**Solow-Swan Model (Exponential):**
- Equation: y = 2068.3e^(0.0577x)
- R² = 0.988

**Polynomial Model:**
- Equation: y = 5.8366x² + 235.39x + 1616.6
- R² = 0.9977

### Key Insights

**Three Stages of Economic Transformation:**
1. **Export Processing Zones (1970s)** - Labor-intensive
2. **Heavy Industrialization (1980s)** - Capital-intensive
3. **Semiconductor Industry (1990s-2020s)** - Technology-intensive

**Model Limitations:**
- The "Taiwan Miracle" of 1970s-80s (>8% annual growth) artificially inflates projections for 2020+
- Residual analysis shows systematic deviations during structural transformation periods

### Residual Analysis

The Solow-Swan model assumes constant growth rates but fails to capture:
- **Positive residuals** (model underestimates): Technology breakthroughs, industrial upgrading
- **Negative residuals** (model overestimates): Economic crises, adjustment periods
- Significant negative residuals during 2008 financial crisis and 2020 pandemic

## 💡 Conclusions

### Two Types of Convergence: The Dialog

| Convergence Type | Test Result | Policy Implications |
|-----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **β-Convergence (Catch-up Effect)** |  Not Found | Poverty does not automatically lead to fast growth |
| **σ-Convergence (Gap Narrowing)** |  Not Found | Gaps show widening tendency |

### Overall Picture: Divergence, Not Convergence

**Why No Convergence?**

1. **Technology Gaps Are Hard to Bridge**
   - Singapore and South Korea continue to push the technological frontier
   - Czech Republic faces "middle-income trap" risks

2. **Structural Transformation Is Critical**
   - Taiwan case: From OEM to independent innovation (TSMC)
   - Capital accumulation alone is insufficient for sustained growth

3. **Global Shocks Amplify Gaps**
   - 2008 financial crisis, 2020 pandemic
   - Recovery speeds differ significantly across economies

4. **Institutional and Policy Factors**
   - Education investment, R&D expenditure
   - Industrial policy, degree of openness

## 🔍 Theoretical Reflections

### Limitations of the Solow-Swan Model

**Strengths:**
- Provides clear analytical framework
- Long-term trend prediction is accurate (R² ≈ 0.99)

**Limitations:**
- Assumes exogenous and constant technological progress
- Ignores structural transformation and breakthroughs
- Cannot explain divergence phenomena

### Extensions for Future Research

1. **Endogenous Growth Theory**
   - Human capital investment (Mankiw-Romer-Weil, 1992)
   - R&D and innovation

2. **Structural Transformation Models**
   - Industrial upgrading pathways
   - Technology leapfrogging

3. **Institutional Economics Perspective**
   - Property rights protection
   - Government quality
